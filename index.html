Microscopic Examination

Gram Staining

  • Primary classification method for bacteria based on cell wall structure
  • Gram Positive: Appears blue/purple (thick cell wall)
  • Gram Negative: Appears red/pink (thin cell wall)
  • Most important staining technique in bacteriology

Acid Fast Staining

  • Special stain to detect acid-fast bacteria
  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Tuberculosis)
  • Mycobacterium leprae (Leprosy)
  • Bacteria appear red on blue background

About Serological Tests

Serological tests detect antibodies or antigens to identify bacterial infections. These tests are based on antigen-antibody reactions and are crucial for diagnosing infections where the pathogen is difficult to culture or when rapid diagnosis is needed.

Common Types of Serological Tests:

  • ELISA: Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay
  • Agglutination Tests: Direct and Indirect
  • Immunofluorescence: Direct and Indirect IF
  • Western Blot: Protein detection method

Phage Typing

About Phage Typing

  • Using bacteriophages (bacterial viruses) to identify and classify bacterial strains
  • This method exploits the specificity of bacteriophages for particular bacterial strains, making it useful for epidemiological studies and outbreak investigations

Principle & Applications

  • Principle: Specific phages lyse specific bacterial strains
  • Applications: Epidemiological investigations
  • Advantages: High specificity and reproducibility
  • Common uses: Staphylococcus aureus, Salmonella typing

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

DNA amplification technique for bacterial identification at molecular level. PCR is one of the most powerful tools in modern microbiology, allowing for rapid and accurate detection of bacterial pathogens even in small quantities.

Types of PCR:

  • Real-Time PCR (qPCR): Quantitative detection
  • Multiplex PCR: Multiple targets simultaneously
  • Reverse Transcription PCR: For RNA detection
  • Nested PCR: Enhanced specificity

Basic media (general-purpose media)

  • Basal media are simple media that support most non-fastidious bacteria.
  • Examples: Peptone-water, nutrient broth, nutrient agar.
  • Used for primary isolation of microorganisms.

Enriched media

  • Extra nutrients such as blood, serum, egg yolk added to basal medium.
  • Used to grow fastidious bacteria.
  • Examples: Blood agar, Chocolate agar, Loefflerâ€™s serum slope.
  • Blood agar: 5-10% blood added to agar base.
  • Chocolate agar: heated/lysed blood agar.

Selective media

  • Suppress growth of some microorganisms while allowing others to grow.
  • Agar-based medium to isolate individual colonies.
  • Examples:
    • Mannitol Salt Agar & Salt Milk Agar: recover S. aureus (10% NaCl).
    • MacConkeyâ€™s Agar: Enterobacteriaceae (bile salts inhibit Gram-positive).
    • Pseudosel Agar (Cetrimide Agar): recover P. aeruginosa.
    • Crystal Violet Blood Agar: recover S. pyogenes.
    • Lowenstein-Jensen Medium: recover M. tuberculosis or M. bovis (malachite green selective).

Enrichment media

  • Increase relative concentration of certain microorganisms before plating.
  • Typically used as broth medium.
  • Examples: Selenite F broth, Tetrathionate broth, Alkaline peptone water (for pathogen recovery).

Differential (indicator) media

  • Distinguish bacteria based on colony color or behavior.
  • Uses dyes, metabolic substrates, or other ingredients.
  • Examples:
    • Mannitol Salt Agar (yellow colonies = mannitol fermentation).
    • Blood Agar (Î±, Î², Î³ hemolysis).
    • MacConkey Agar (lactose fermenters = pink colonies; non-fermenters = colorless).

Transport media

  • Used to transport samples to laboratory.
  • Prevents overgrowth of contaminating organisms.
  • Maintains pathogen/commensal ratio, prevents drying.

Anaerobic media

  • Used for bacteria that need low oxygen tension and extra nutrients.
  • Additives: 1% glucose, 0.1% thioglycollate, 0.1% ascorbic acid, 0.05% cysteine.
  • Medium boiled and sealed with sterile paraffin before use.
  • Examples:
    • Robertson Cooked Meat (RCM) medium for Clostridium spp.
    • Thioglycollate broth (sodium thioglycollate, glucose, cystine, yeast extract, casein hydrolysate).

Assay media

  • Used for assay of vitamins, amino acids, antibiotics.
  • Determines antibiotic potency by microbiological assay.
  • Example: Mueller-Hinton agar.

Other types of media

  • Media for enumeration of bacteria: Plate count agar, Levine's eosin-methylene blue (L-EMB) agar.
  • Media for characterization: Triple sugar iron agar.
  • Maintenance media etc.

Catalase Test

Catalase test is used to detect the presence of catalase enzyme in bacteria.

Procedure:

  • CATALASE TEST
  • Purpose:
    • Differentiate members of the family Micrococcaceae (including Staphylococcus) which are catalase positive from Streptococcus species which are catalase negative.
    • Differentiate Listeria monocytogenes and Corynebacteria (catalase positive) from other Gram-positive, non-spore-forming bacilli.
  • Principle:
    • The enzyme catalase catalyzes the release of water and oxygen from hydrogen peroxide.
    • Chemical reaction: 2 H2O2 â€”â€”â€”â†’ 2 H2O + Nascent Oxygen
  • Interpretation:
    • Positive: Rapid and sustained appearance of bubbles or effervescence.

Coagulase Test

Coagulase test identifies the ability of bacteria to clot plasma.

Procedure:

  • COAGULASE TEST
  • Purpose:
    • Determine the ability of the organism to produce coagulase, which clots plasma.
    • Distinguish the pathogenic coagulase-positive Staphylococcus from the nonpathogenic coagulase-negative Staphylococcus.
  • Principle:
    • Coagulase is an enzyme that converts soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin.
    • Two forms of coagulase:
      • Bound coagulase (clumping factor): Detected in the coagulase slide test. Can directly convert fibrinogen to insoluble fibrin and causes staphylococci to clump together.
      • Free coagulase: Detected in the coagulase tube test. Reacts with a globulin plasma factor (coagulase reacting factor - CRF) to form a thrombin-like factor, staphylothrombin â†’ catalyzes the conversion of fibrinogen to insoluble fibrin.
  • Interpretation:
    • Slide Coagulase test: Positive - white fibrin clots in plasma; Negative - smooth suspension.
    • Tube Coagulase test: Positive - formation of fibrin clot; Negative - no clot is formed.

Oxidase Test

Oxidase test detects the presence of cytochrome c oxidase enzyme in bacteria.

Procedure:

  • OXIDASE TEST
  • Purpose:
    • Screen colonies suspected of being one of the Enterobacteriaceae (all negative).
    • Identify colonies suspected of belonging to other genera such as Aeromonas, Pseudomonas, Neisseria, Campylobacter, and Pasteurella.
  • Principle:
    • The cytochrome oxidase test uses certain reagent dyes, such as p-phenylenediamine dihydrochloride, that substitute for oxygen as artificial electron acceptors. It is colorless in the reduced state.
    • In the presence of cytochrome oxidase and atmospheric oxygen, p-phenylenediamine is oxidized, forming indophenol blue.

TSI Test

Triple Sugar Iron (TSI) test differentiates bacteria based on carbohydrate fermentation and H2S production.

Procedure:

  • TRIPLE SUGAR IRON (TSI) TEST
  • Purpose:
    • As an initial step in the identification of Enterobacteriaceae.
  • Principle:
    • The action of many species of microorganisms on a carbohydrate substrate results in the acidification of the medium with or without gas formation.
    • Iron salts (ferrous sulfate and ferric ammonium citrate) react with H2S to produce an insoluble black precipitate (ferrous sulfide).
    • H2S gas is produced by some bacteria such as Salmonella and reacts with the iron salts incorporated into the TSI agar.
  • Composition:
    • Protein sources: beef extract, peptone, yeast extract, proteose peptone
    • Sugars: lactose, sucrose, glucose
    • Indicators:
      • Phenol red: carbohydrate fermentation
      • Ferrous sulfate: hydrogen sulfide production
      • Sodium thiosulfate: source of sulfur atoms
      • Sodium chloride: osmotic stabilizer
